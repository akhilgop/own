My Process

Step 01

Discover

The first stage of the design process is to understand the perspective of the target audience/customer/consumer to identify and address the problem at hand. To do this, I encouraged to cast aside all assumptions (because assumptions can stifle innovation!) about the problem, the consumers, and the world at large. This allows me to objectively consider any and all possibilities about the customers and their needs. after this phase, we'll get solution for the below questions,

  • What is the executive intent?
  • Who are the target users?
  • That general tasks will it perform?
  • What are the technology constraints?
  • What is the critical success factor?
  • What are the cross channels?
Step 02

Define

At this stage, I'll analyze the qualitative and quantitative data we obtained during the empathize stage to draw insights from it. This information will be used both to define a problem statement and guide the ideation process. Clear problem statements will guide me throughout the design process, those statements will help me to understand what features and functions my users need in order to solve their problems. the following processess will done after this phase

  • User Journey map creation
  • Information Architecture
Step 03

Ideate

Ideation, or generating ideas, is a phase in which Ive to go from understanding problems to exploring solutions. The ideas that are identified will be prototyped and tested with people who represent my target audience. Brainstorming, mind-mapping, landscape mapping and Post-it Notes are all viable tools to fuel this brilliant but messy phase. The key is to create an environment where divergent and provocative options are embraced and assessed, with the eventual goal of converging on a few strongest pathways to pursue.

Step 04

Prototyping

Prototyping allows you to turn ideas generated in the previous phase into tangible artifacts that can be tested later with real users. Most of the time, you start with low-fidelity prototypes (prototypes that convey the basic idea of the intended solution) and move towards high-fidelity as you get more user feedback. these are the common techniques during the prototyping phase:

  • Paper prototyping: Paper prototypes can help quickly build and validate the design hypothesis with minimal effort. I will uild rough paper prototypes to find what’s working and what’s not.
  • High-fidelity digital prototyping: Hi-fi prototypes are great for validating user flows and identify areas that require further attention. we can collect more detailed feedback using hi-fi prototypes..
Step 05

Testing

The goal of this phase is to understand what parts of the design are effective and which are not. During the testing phase, the product team gives a prototype to test participants and encourages them to complete some common tasks with it. Testing will give a product team a clearer understanding of how real users interact with a product, what problems they face, and how they feel. these are the useful techniques during the test phase:

  • Moderated usability testing: I'll conduct a series of testing sessions with my solution so you can get feedback from people who represent my target audience. Moderators can ask clarifying questions and collect more detailed feedback from participants (i.e., why exactly do test participants act the way they do).
  • Focus Groups are typically small groups of people (six to nine participants) who come together to review and discuss a particular solution. Focus groups are great when you have a specific topic you want to explore (say, understanding how user onboarding makes users feel).
